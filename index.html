Neon Dungeon Inventory Automation Demo

A proof-of-concept operations system for a fictional merch store.

In this scenario, Neon Dungeon is an escape room with a new merch line: shirts, mugs, and a custom-made board game based on the unique setting.

For the owner, merch should be exciting. It extends the brand, deepens the guest experience, and creates a new revenue stream. But every new product also adds operational details: stock counts, fulfillment rules, reorder thresholds, protected venue inventory, and follow-up tasks.

The owner did not open an escape room to spend their time tracking inventory.

This prototype shows how those operational details can be modeled in Google Sheets and routed through Make, turning fragmented follow-up work into a clearer workflow.

The Problem

Small businesses often grow by adding new ideas before they have formal systems to support them.

A merch line sounds simple at first: sell a shirt, sell a mug, sell a board game. But the details multiply quickly. Some products are print-on-demand. Some ship from venue stock. Some inventory needs to be protected for in-person guests. Some items need reorder thresholds. Someone still has to notice when action is needed.

Without a clear workflow, the creative part of the business gets pulled toward repetitive operational checking.

The Prototype

This demo uses three elegant pieces: A customer-facing storefront prototype, a Google Sheets inventory tracker, and a Make automation workflow.

The goal is not to build a production checkout system yet. The goal is to map the workflow, expose the business rules, and show how automation can protect the creative work at the center of the business.

1. Inventory Tracker

Google Sheets acts as the operational source of truth for product stock, fulfillment rules, reorder thresholds, protected minimums, and low-stock flags.

The sheet separates products by how they should be handled. Shirts and mugs can route to print-on-demand for online orders, while the board game uses venue stock above a protected minimum.

2. Storefront Prototype

The storefront shows how the merch line appears to a customer: branded products, variant options, prices, and fulfillment labels.

This gives the business owner a visible prototype of the customer experience before investing in production infrastructure.

3. Demo Cart

The demo cart shows a customer order for one Neon Dungeon Board Game.

The board game is used for the inventory example because it depends on venue stock. That makes it a better test case than print-on-demand products, where online orders would not reduce venue inventory.

4. Inventory Reflection

For the prototype handoff, the sample order is reflected in the inventory tracker.

The board game row tracks the current venue stock, protected minimum, pending online orders, available online quantity, reorder threshold, and fulfillment rule. These are the operational details that need to stay visible as the merch line grows.

5. Automation Workflow

Make checks the board game inventory data in Google Sheets, aggregates the relevant quantity values, and sends the result to an HTTP endpoint.

This represents the operational handoff: once inventory data is checked, the system can trigger a notification, reorder action, vendor follow-up, or staff message.

What This Proves

This project demonstrates a workflow pattern that can apply beyond merch inventory.

This specific example is based on merchandise sold by an escape room. The larger skill is translating fragmented business operations into clear, repeatable systems.

This prototype shows the ability to:

  • Design a customer-facing interface.
  • Model inventory and fulfillment rules.
  • Use spreadsheets as lightweight operational databases.
  • Connect business data to an automation layer.
  • Explain the workflow visually for non-technical users.

Where This Could Go Next

The same pattern could support low-stock alerts, vendor reorder emails, Discord or SMS staff notifications, escape room booking alerts, equipment inspection logs, customer follow-up messages, and daily operations reports.

For a small business, the value is not automation for its own sake. The value is getting routine follow-up out of the owner’s head and into a system that can be checked, improved, and expanded.

Prototype Scope

This is a proof of concept, not a production checkout system.

Some handoffs are simulated to keep the workflow clear. The goal is to demonstrate the business logic, tool integration, and automation pattern before building production infrastructure.
